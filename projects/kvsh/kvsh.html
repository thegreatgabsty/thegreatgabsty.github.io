work

KVSH chocolate

This project was created for Design for Good at RISD under the instruction of Annalisa Oswald during Wintersession 2018.

For this assignment, we were to design packaging for a chocolate that helped a social cause in some way. I designed KVSH Chocolate, a fictional company that produces THC chocolate and donates its profits to the American Cancer Foundation and helps provide free cannabis products to chemo patients.

My thinking was that alcohol often has the best branding practices, why not apply fun packaging concepts to the growing market of cannabis products?

Sketches

I always start any project out with hand sketches; I find it's best to start away from the computer altogether. These are a few pages out of my sketchbook from my brainstorming period for this project. I began by thinking about my consumer audience and the mood or feeling I wanted my brand to communicate. I wondered what kind of graphic language was appropriate to exhibit this feeling.

I initially created concepts for abstractions of marijuana leaves and the THC molecule so as to branch out from the concrete concept into a broader spectrum of design ideas.

Concept Evolution

I took to the computer next, iterating and bouncing ideas off each other in Illustrator. Pictured below are several snapshots of my artboards in a sort of timeline.

I wanted this brand to be completely transparent about what it was about, but for the brand NOT to scream "THIS IS WEED" like how Kiva bars do. This brand is for the discreet stoner who believes in doing good.

Final Packaging

I finalized a packaging design that features bold colors and simple but whimsical pattern work. I emphasized on the idea of brain activity, neurotransmitters and brain waves through the use of scattered symbols and white wavy lines.

To solidify the idea of truth in simplicity, I made the bottom half scientific and similar to a periodic table chart; all the information is laid out clean and easy to digest. I feel I was successful in communicating a feeling of stimulation, playfulness, and flow.

The final packaging was printed on bristol velum and blocks of wood simulated the chocolate inside.
